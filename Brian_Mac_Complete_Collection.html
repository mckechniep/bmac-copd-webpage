So, what is the answer? It is really quite sensible. Eat several small meals throughout the day. If you are at or below ideal body weight, eat foods that are high in calories. Stem Cells / Regenerative Therapies for COPD

Currently, regenerative therapies are divided into extrinsic therapeutic strategies and intrinsic cell therapy methods. There has been some work looking at the potential to bioengineer fully intact 3-D lung units that could be transplanted but this is not a likely prospect for the near future. Extrinsic cell therapy refers to infusing (or endotracheal installation) of stem cells including embryonic stem cells (ESCs), induced pleuripotent stem cells (iPSs), mesenchymal stem cells (MSCs), and human lung stem cells (hLSCs). Intrinsic therapy refers to the delivery of small molecules (retinoid compounds have been the most studied) that can stimulate the endogenous lung stem/progenitor cells to regenerate and replace damaged structures (See Sun et al review paper below).

PMC: Stem cell and regenerative therapies review (PMC6190524)
Note: There are several companies developing stem cell therapies for COPD, some notable ones in NJ.

A Novel Approach to Acute COPD [Azithromycin]

LOS ANGELES (Sept. 6, 2011) -- Richard Casaburi, Ph.D., M.D., principal investigator at Los Angeles Biomedical Research Institute (LA BioMed), is co-author of a recent study that should help improve the condition for the millions of individuals who suffer from chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, better known as COPD. The results of the study reveal that adding a common antibiotic - azithromycin - to the usual daily treatment for COPD reduced the occurrence of acute exacerbations by 27% and tended to improve the quality of life for COPD patients. The results of the study, funded by the National Heart, Lung, and Blood Institute (NHLBI), part of the National Institutes of Health, appeared in the Aug. 25 issue of the New England Journal of Medicine.

"This important study adds to a growing number of approaches that reduce the frequency of COPD illnesses," said Dr. Casaburi. "Together, they promise COPD patients who avail themselves of readily-available therapies substantially reduced risk of acute lung illnesses. This has the potential to reduce the misery this disease brings and can also can bring down medical costs, as more than half of COPD costs are related to hospitalization for COPD-related illnesses."

At 10 regional centers around the United States, the study recruited 558 study participants who took 250 mg of azithromycin daily for a year, in addition to their usual care. They averaged 1.48 acute COPD exacerbations annually, compared to 1.83 exacerbations for the 559 participants who received traditional care without azithromycin. The participants taking azithromycin also tended to report more favorable breathing ability and overall well-being.

Terms & Definitions

Spirometry (Pulmonary Function Testing)

Spirometry is the core lung function test used to:

  • Diagnose COPD
  • Differentiate COPD from asthma
  • Grade disease severity

How the Test Works

  1. Patient inhales fully.
  2. Then forcefully exhales into a spirometer.
  3. Test is repeated before and after a bronchodilator.

Bronchodilator (BD) / Post‚ÄëBD

  • Bronchodilator (BD): medication (commonly albuterol) that relaxes airway smooth muscle and opens airways.
  • Post‚ÄëBD: measurements taken after bronchodilator administration (usually 10‚Äì15 minutes later).
Why it matters: asthma improves significantly post‚ÄëBD, while COPD obstruction persists; diagnosis is based on post‚ÄëBD values.

FEV1 (Forced Expiratory Volume in 1 Second)

  • Volume of air exhaled in the first second
  • Measured in liters
  • Reflects airway obstruction severity (lower FEV1 = worse limitation)

FVC (Forced Vital Capacity)

  • Total air exhaled after full inhalation
  • Measured in liters
  • Used with FEV1 to calculate obstruction

FEV1 / FVC Ratio

  • Normal lungs: ‚â• 0.70‚Äì0.80
  • Obstructed lungs: ratio drops
  • Post‚ÄëBD FEV1/FVC < 0.70 = airflow obstruction ‚Üí COPD confirmed
  • This is a fixed ratio, not a percentage

Confirmed COPD (Definition)

  • Spirometry performed
  • Bronchodilator given
  • Obstruction persists below cutoff
  • COPD diagnosis is formally established

FEV1 % Predicted

Your measured FEV1 compared to a normal reference value for someone of the same age, sex, height, and ethnicity.

Formula: FEV1 % predicted = (Measured FEV1 √∑ Predicted normal FEV1) √ó 100

Example: Predicted 4.0 L, measured 1.6 L ‚Üí 40% predicted

  • Roflumilast is considered when COPD is severe (often FEV1 < 50%).
  • Chronic bronchitis phenotype.

Marked ‚Äúoptional‚Äù because severity may already be clinically obvious or spirometry may be outdated/unavailable.

Roflumilast

  • Oral PDE‚Äë4 inhibitor
  • Reduces airway inflammation
  • Not a bronchodilator
  • Used to reduce exacerbations, not symptoms

Common side effects: weight loss, diarrhea, nausea, anxiety/depression.

LABA / LAMA / ICS

  • LABA: Long‚Äëacting beta‚Äëagonist (bronchodilator). Examples: formoterol, salmeterol.
  • LAMA: Long‚Äëacting muscarinic antagonist (bronchodilator). Examples: tiotropium, umeclidinium.
  • ICS: Inhaled corticosteroid (anti‚Äëinflammatory). Increases pneumonia risk in some COPD patients.

Trelegy

  • Trelegy Ellipta = triple therapy inhaler (LABA + LAMA + ICS)
  • Once daily
  • Used in moderate‚Äìsevere COPD with exacerbations

Air Trapping

  • Inability to fully exhale air
  • Leads to lung hyperinflation
  • Causes dyspnea and exercise intolerance
  • Seen in: COPD, emphysema, obstructive lung disease

Alpha‚Äë1 Antitrypsin Deficiency (AATD)

  • Genetic disorder (SERPINA1 mutation)
  • Deficiency of alpha‚Äë1 antitrypsin protein
  • Consequences: early emphysema (often lower lobes), liver disease, COPD at young age with minimal smoking
  • Why screen: all COPD patients should be tested at least once

NTM (Nontuberculous Mycobacteria)

  • Environmental mycobacteria (water, soil)
  • Not tuberculosis
  • Common species: MAC (Mycobacterium avium complex)
  • Can colonize lungs without causing disease

ATS / IDSA Criteria (for NTM Lung Disease)

Published by the American Thoracic Society and Infectious Diseases Society of America to distinguish true disease from colonization.

All three required:

  • Clinical: chronic cough, sputum, fatigue, weight loss, dyspnea
  • Radiographic (CT chest): bronchiectasis, nodules, cavities, tree‚Äëin‚Äëbud pattern
  • Microbiologic: ‚â• 2 positive sputum cultures, or ‚â• 1 positive bronchoscopic sample, or biopsy + culture

‚Äú3% Met ATS/IDSA Criteria for NTM Disease‚Äù

  • Many people had NTM detected
  • Only ~3% met full criteria
  • The rest likely had colonization or contamination

Culture Conversion

Lab cultures go from positive ‚Üí negative, indicating microbiologic treatment success. Often used as a primary outcome in NTM treatment.

Refractory Disease

  • Disease fails to respond to appropriate therapy
  • Persistent positive cultures
  • Ongoing symptoms

Localized Disease

Disease confined to one lung region, making surgical treatment possible.

Surgical Resection (NTM Context)

  • Removal of affected lung segment or lobe
  • Used only for localized, refractory disease
  • Combined with antibiotics
  • Reported outcomes: ~93% culture conversion in selected patients

Macrolides

Antibiotic class: azithromycin, clarithromycin, erythromycin. Used in COPD (anti‚Äëinflammatory) and as core drugs in NTM treatment.

Acquired Macrolide Resistance

  • Resistance that develops after exposure
  • Often due to monotherapy or long‚Äëterm low‚Äëdose use
  • Once present, macrolides may become ineffective permanently
  • Major problem in NTM disease

Rifampin

Antibiotic used in TB/NTM regimens; strong enzyme inducer.

CYP450 Enzymes

Liver enzymes that metabolize drugs and control drug blood levels.

Rifampin Induces CYP450; May Affect COPD Medications

  • Speeds up drug metabolism
  • Other medications may drop to sub‚Äëtherapeutic levels or become ineffective
  • Especially relevant for roflumilast, oral steroids, some ICS (systemic exposure), and cardiac/anticoagulant meds

Mental Model Summary

  • Spirometry ‚Üí diagnosis + severity
  • FEV1/FVC ‚Üí confirms COPD
  • FEV1 % predicted ‚Üí grades severity
  • Roflumilast ‚Üí severe, exacerbation‚Äëprone COPD
  • NTM ‚Üí strict ATS/IDSA criteria before treatment
  • Macrolides ‚Üí powerful but resistance‚Äëprone
  • Rifampin ‚Üí major drug interaction risk
  • AATD ‚Üí genetic COPD cause that must be ruled out

Colonization vs Contamination vs ‚ÄúMeets Full ATS/IDSA Criteria‚Äù

These three terms describe very different meanings of a positive NTM result. Same lab finding ‚Üí very different clinical significance.

Contamination

  • NTM never came from the patient‚Äôs lungs
  • Entered the sample by accident (tap water exposure, improper collection, lab handling artifacts)
  • Key features: single positive culture, no symptoms, normal or non‚Äësuggestive imaging, never reproducible
  • Clinical meaning: false signal ‚Üí ignore it
  • Mental model: ‚ÄúThe bug fell into the cup.‚Äù

Colonization

  • NTM present in airways but not causing active disease
  • Common because NTM live in water/soil and damaged lungs are easy to inhabit
  • Key features: 1‚Äì2 positive sputum cultures, mild/no symptoms, imaging not clearly progressive
  • Does not meet microbiologic + clinical + radiographic criteria together
  • Clinical meaning: watch, don‚Äôt treat (monitor symptoms, imaging, repeat cultures)
  • Treating here causes toxicity, resistance, and no benefit
  • Mental model: ‚ÄúThe bug lives there, but isn‚Äôt attacking.‚Äù

Meets Full ATS/IDSA Criteria (True NTM Disease)

Defined by the ATS/IDSA to prevent overtreatment. All three must be present:

  • Clinical: chronic cough, sputum, fatigue, weight loss, dyspnea, symptoms unexplained by another cause
  • Radiographic (CT chest): bronchiectasis, nodules, cavities, tree‚Äëin‚Äëbud opacities; progressive or characteristic patterns matter
  • Microbiologic: ‚â• 2 positive sputum cultures, or ‚â• 1 positive bronchoscopy culture, or lung biopsy with histology + culture

Clinical meaning: real disease; treatment is justified; long, multi‚Äëdrug regimens are warranted; outcomes tracked using culture conversion.

Mental model: ‚ÄúThe bug is present, active, damaging tissue, and persistent.‚Äù

Putting the ‚Äú3%‚Äù Statement in Context

  • 100% ‚Üí NTM detected somewhere
  • ~97% ‚Üí contamination or colonization
  • ~3% ‚Üí true disease requiring treatment

This is intentional: NTM treatment lasts 18‚Äì24 months, uses toxic antibiotics, and resistance is permanent once induced. Over‚Äëtreatment causes real harm.

COPD & Cold Weather: What's Going On

Cold weather is a major COPD exacerbator. Here's what the research shows:

Key Mechanisms

  • Facial cooling reflex (primary): Cooling facial skin (even without breathing cold air) can trigger bronchoconstriction. This affects COPD patients and healthy people alike, causing roughly a 9‚Äì10% FEV1 decrease with whole-body exposure to -17¬∞C.
  • Direct airway effects (secondary): During exertion, cold dry air hits the lower airways, dehydrating airway surface liquid and triggering inflammation and smooth muscle contraction. COPD patients can see an additional ~8% FEV1 drop.
  • TRPM8 cold receptor overexpression: COPD patients (especially smokers) overexpress TRPM8 cold-sensing receptors, making them hypersensitive to cold triggers. Even air conditioning can provoke symptoms.

What the Data Shows

  • A 10¬∞F (5.5¬∞C) drop in daily minimum temperature correlates with increased rescue inhaler use, a 0.17 rise in BCSS symptom score, and mean decreases of 38 mL and 26 mL in morning/evening FEV1.
  • Patients are susceptible despite limited time outdoors, suggesting even brief cold exposure matters.
  • Seasonal patterns show higher COPD exacerbation rates in winter months.

Sources: PubMed Central.

Dyspnea (No Mucus) Recommendations

Dyspnea Without Mucus

With emphysema‚Äëpredominant COPD (no mucus), the problem isn't secretions blocking airways ‚Äî it's air trapping, hyperinflation, and weakened respiratory muscles. OPEP devices and mucolytics won't help much.

Below are evidence‚Äëbased breathing techniques and devices that improve symptoms.

The Core Problem

Dynamic hyperinflation results in a rapid, shallow breathing pattern: inspiratory muscles shorten faster, causing functional weakness, reduced dynamic lung compliance, and worse gas exchange. PubMed Central

Goal: reduce air trapping, improve breathing efficiency, and strengthen respiratory muscles.

Tier 1: Breathing Techniques (Free)

Pursed‚ÄëLip Breathing (PLB) ‚Äî Best Evidence

PLB alters respiratory mechanics by creating back‚Äëpressure (extrinsic PEEP), reducing airway collapse and hyperinflation. Frontiers

How to do it:

  1. Breathe in slowly through the nose for 2 counts.
  2. Purse lips like you're going to whistle or blow out a candle.
  3. Exhale slowly through pursed lips for 4‚Äì6 counts.
  4. Exhale time should be 2‚Äì3x longer than inhale.

When to use it:

  • During activities that cause breathlessness.
  • Walking up stairs.
  • During recovery after exertion.
  • When feeling anxious about breathing.

Network meta‚Äëanalysis: yoga (44%) and inspiratory muscle training (22%) ranked highest for dyspnea improvement. PubMed

Forward‚ÄëLeaning Postures

Bracing the arms reduces work of breathing and stabilizes the ribcage. PubMed Central

Positions that help:

  • Sitting forward with elbows on knees.
  • Standing and leaning forward on a counter/railing.
  • Standing with hands on hips (tripod position).
  • Lying on your side with a pillow between knees.

Diaphragmatic Breathing

Reduces sympathetic activity and increases parasympathetic tone. Nature

Evidence is mixed for COPD; PLB and IMT have stronger support. PubMed

Bottom line: PLB is better proven than diaphragmatic breathing for emphysema patients.

Tier 2: Inspiratory Muscle Training (IMT)

IMT strengthens breathing muscles and improves dyspnea and quality of life. PubMed Central

8 weeks of IMT improves inspiratory muscle strength/endurance and exercise capacity. POWERbreathe
